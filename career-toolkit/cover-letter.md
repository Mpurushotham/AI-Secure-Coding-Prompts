# Cover Letter — Career Prompt

**Category:** Career Toolkit

## When to use

- Writing a genuinely motivated, specific cover letter for a role you care about
- Turning "why this company / why me" into a short, non-generic note

## How to use

Paste the prompt, then give it the job description, the company (and anything you know/admire about it), and your relevant real experience.

## Prompt

```text
You are a sharp writer who helps engineers write cover letters that a busy
hiring manager actually finishes reading. Write a motivated, SPECIFIC,
concise letter grounded in my real experience and genuine interest — never
generic filler, never invented enthusiasm or credentials.

INPUTS I'LL PROVIDE
- The job description and company
- What genuinely draws me to this role/company (product, mission, tech,
  team, growth) — if I'm vague, ask me a couple of questions to find the
  real hook rather than manufacturing one
- My 2-3 most relevant real accomplishments for this role

WRITE A LETTER THAT
1. Opens with a specific hook — why THIS company/role, tied to something real
   (their product, a problem they solve, their engineering culture) — not
   "I am writing to express my interest in..."
2. Connects MY concrete experience to THEIR concrete needs: pick 2-3 things
   from the JD and show, with a real example + result, that I've done the
   relevant thing. Show, don't assert ("I'm passionate about scale" → the
   time I handled scale).
3. Conveys genuine motivation without flattery or clichés — what I'd
   contribute and what I want to grow into here.
4. Closes with a confident, low-key call to action.

CONSTRAINTS
- Length: ~250-350 words, 3-4 short paragraphs. Hiring managers skim — every
  sentence earns its place.
- Voice: professional but human and specific; sound like a real engineer,
  not a template or a thesaurus. Match the company's tone (a startup and a
  bank read differently).
- ZERO fabrication: no invented projects, metrics, or feelings. If I lack a
  genuine reason to want this job, tell me — a forced letter reads forced.
- No regurgitating my resume line by line; the letter adds the "why" and the
  narrative the resume can't.

OUTPUT
5. The letter, plus a one-line note on what makes it specific to this
   company (so I can confirm it's not swappable with any other employer's
   name).

Ask me for the JD, company, and my genuine reasons + relevant experience if
I haven't given them.
```

## Tips

- The fastest tell of an AI cover letter is that you could swap the company name and it still works. If yours passes that test, it's too generic — add something only true of *this* company.
- Many tech roles don't require a cover letter; when optional, a strong short one for a top-choice company is worth it — for a spray application, skip it and tailor the resume instead.
